Understanding the external accounts
  • Two accounts represent external position of a country
    • Balance of Payment (BOP)
      • Flow of transactions (imports, exports, borrowing, investment)
    • Net International Investment Position (NIIP)
      • Stock of foreign assets owned by residents - stock of domestic assets owned by nonresidents
      • Measured at end of period
  • Must account for change in valuation of stock in addition to flow
    • An flow can be net zero but the stock of assets flucuated due to one currency appreciating against the foreign partner.
  • Often presented as ratios to GDP
  • Residency rather than citizenship matters
  • Genral government is defined as domestic resident
  • recored on accrual basis at market prices
  • merchandise exports/imports recorded on "free on board". excluding shipping and unsurance cost
  • payments for transportation and insurance recorded in the services account
  • For guide in classification - IMF's Balance of Payments and International Investment Position Manual
Balance of Payments (Flows)

CAB + KAB + NFB + E&O + RT = 0

  • trade in goods and services, factor income, anf financing of transactions
  • starting point for analyzing a countries relationship w/ ROW
  • Five main BOP groups:
    • Current account balance (CAB) (FRED: IEABC)
      • summarizes the balance transactions in goods and services and income flows
      • Includes:
        • trade account: receipts of goods exports - payments for goods imports
        • services account: receipts of services exports - payments for services imports
          • transportation, insurance, tourism, legal, medical services
        • primary (net) income flows: net income from temp labor, financial sources or assets
          • labor income from residents temp abroad, interest, dividends, retained earnings, rental income on foreign property, grants/aides
        • secondary income flows (current transfers): gifts in kind, remittances, payments to meet obligations to internation orgs.
      • Many times gross imports and exports are shown along w/ trade balance. Others are shown just in net terms
        • Gross Import FRED: IEAMGS
        • Gross Export FRED: IEAXGS
        • Gross Income FRED: IEAXI
    • Capital account balance (KAB) (FRED: IEABCP)
      • Includes:
        • Unilateral transfers from or to non-residents that change the stock of the reciept country's financial/real assets
          • development grants intended for fixed capital formation, debt forgivness intended for improvment rather than reorginization of debt
        • Aqusition/disposal of nonproduced/ nonfinancial assets
          • ownership of land, mineral resources, leases, marketing assets such as brands
    • Financial Account - Net financial balance excluding changes in official reserve holdings (NFB) (FRED: IEANLF)
      • transactions/exchange in financial instruments
      • can be charaterized in many ways but most common is a functional way to identify specifics charaterisitcs of financial flows and their sustanability
        • Foreign direct investment (FDI)
          • residents make an investment that gives an individual/business control/influence over the management of an enterprise in another country
          • significant control mean voting share of greater than 10 percent
          • includes debts flows from investor to enterprise if enterprise is direct investment affliate
        • Portfolio Investment
          • transactions in debt or equity securities other than those included in direct investment/reserve assets
          • easy to trade assets with liquid markets
        • Financial derivatives (other than reserves) and employee stock options
          • financial transactions w/ that are motivated by risk transfers rather than investment of funds
          • forward contracts and options trading are large portion of this
          • stock options are cross-boarder compensation and are included due to the option feature
          • often included in "Other investments" during presentations
        • Other investments
          • residual transactions not included in previous categories
          • direct lending/borrowing, trade credits, currency transactions, IMF credits, special drawing rights (SDR), nonsecuritize equity of < 10%
    • Net reserves loss of the central bank (RT)
      • changes in the reserve positon of the monetary authority that are avaiable for BOP financing and has influence on exchange rate
      • usually equal to the change in net foriegn assets in the central bank accounts w/ opposite sign
    • Net errors and omissions, sum statistical discrepancies + other balances (E&O) (FRED: IEASD)
      • Balancing item. In thoery CAB+KAB+NFB+RT should = 0
  • Overal Balance is usually shown as an aggragate balancing items by shifting RT to right side of equation below and switching the sign
  • ***Important for countries that fix exchange rate because it shows how much FX central bank has to buy/sell to meet net supply/demand for FX
  • overall balance for free floating FX rates is likly to be close to zero. Differing mainly on interest earning on central bank reserves

Net International Investment Position (Stock)

  • an inclusive statement of a country's net claims on nonresidents
  • Balance sheets of sectors
    • government, financial and non financial businesses, and households
  • helps to understand how the flows affect the creditor and debtor positions between domestic and foreignresidents
  • has grown in importance relative to BOP flows recently as the source of economic and financial crises shift from current account to financial accounts.
  • Can find NIIP data in IMF Article IV consultation report
  • stock analysis focuses on two aggregates, External Debt & Foreign Exchange Reserves
    • External Debt
      • consolidation of external debt of all sectors of economy (Households, nonfinancial firms, government, banking system, non bank financial firms)
      • net external debt = residents debt liabilities to nonresidents less nonresidents debt liabilities to residents
      • External debt has higher risk than domestic debt because non residents live in places with differnt laws and politics, etc
      • Exchange rate issues. If debt is borrowed in domestic currency then nonresident is subject to exchange rate risk
      • often a source of vulnerability that can lead to a crisis.
    • Central Bank (Foreign Exchange) Reserves
      • comprise assets thqat are readily available to and controlled by monetary authority for meeting BOP financing needs and influencing the exchange rate
      • ***analysis should be able to judge the adequecy of reserves
      • Gross Reserves

          • Gross Reserves = FXH + MG + SDR + RPF + OA

        • Foreign exchange holdings (FX)
          • in US treasury bills or liquid short term assets
        • Monetary gold holdings (MG)
        • Special drawing rights from IMF (SDR)
        • Reserve positions at the IMF (RPF)
          • foreign currency amount that countries can access
        • Other short-term assets in foriegn current (OA)
          • financial derivatives and central bank lending to non banks and residents
      • Net reserves

          • Gross Reserves - central bank foreign exchange liabilities

External Account Analysis
  • Information from external accounts helps analyze five issues
    • domestic growth, smoothing of economic activity after temporary shocks, competitiveness, repercussions from global imbalances, & vulnerability to crises
  • Domestic Growth
    • for small countries external accounts is part of the country growth model
    • external accounts are also needed to analyze the success of an export-driven growth strategy
    • Metrics
      • ***key measure of market penetration is ratio of country exports to world imports
      • aggregate export compared to world trade growth
      • exports of manufactured goods to world imports of manufactured goods
      • exports of manufactured goods to imports of manufacturing goods in the main market to which the country exports
      • net export in goods and services to growth
        • Contribution of net exports to growth = [(CABt-CABt-1)/GNDIt-1]x 100
    • export growth can drive domestic production and current account surplus can result in an accumulation of financial wealth
  • Domestic Stability
    • equating success with falling current account deficit or rising surplus is misleading
    • large deficits or shriking surplus can help cushion negative shocks
    • external deficits can reflect a country drawing on foreign savings for benifical purposes
    • I = S - CAB
  • Competitiveness
    • we understand competitivness through cost and price measures adjusted by exchange rates
      • aggregate price indices
      • prices of trades good raltive to price of non traded good
      • unit labor cost at home to unit labor cost abroad
    • Real Effective Exchange Rates (REER)
      • the echange rate measure used to adjust
      • shows changes overtime but does not allude to whether level of competitvness is satisfactory
    • Example: if REER is competivite but you have export growth higher than foriegn growth and CAD is widening then you can use external account to understand larger exports.
  • Global Imbalances
    • Understanding the ramification of deficits relative to surpluses in external accounts
    • large deficits matter more than large surplusses
    • all deficits in one country must be matched by a surplus in another
    • Deficits
      • large deficits leasd to a depletion of reserves or a build up of debt, unless financed by FDI inflows
      • this can lead to a debt crises
      • Example:
        • US had large fiscal deficits. savings relative to GDP were falling. theory is that this was the reason for CAD.
    • Surpluses
      • Example:
        • China purchased dollars and invested in US Treasuries. This artificialy held down the remembi and increased current account surplus.
        • China purchases kept down US interest rates that increased demand in US. US imported goods and added to CAD
    • Concern about imbalances
      • imbalances suggested that policy intervention inexchange rates would not adjust to equalibrum.
        • appriaction in surplus countries and depreciate in deficit countries
      • fear that imbalances would eventually adjust and it would be quick and disruptive
        • collapse of dollar senario
    • Tools to use for analysis
      • IMF - External Balance Assesment
      • https://www.imf.org/external/np/res/eba/data.htm
  • Vulnerability to crises
    • most economic crises start from domestic factors and then can spill over into domestic accounts
    • crises can start ib global markets due to:
      • shock to global risk aversion
      • contagian
    • ***external accounts should be analyzed:
      • to asses the possibility of a domestic crises affting external markets,
      • to see if country's external position is adequate to withstand blobal/regional shocks
    • strategy to analyze accounts for vulnerabilities
      • in 1950 countries were small. CAD were financed by offical aide, foreign offical lending, etc. Crises happened when this money ran out
      • since 1970 crises stem from financial accounts. Countries rely on foriegn fianncing for CAD and become indebted to country
    • models of external account crises
      • when country heavily exposed to international capital markets with short term external debt, sentiment can change quickly
      • triggers:
        • change in government, change in expected policy choices, problems at major banks/corps, perceived inability to sustain exchange rate stability, contaigion.
