    CAPM
      Overview

    • CAPM can be used to determine the consensus expected return for an asset.
    • these consensus return are valuable because they give us a standard of comparison.
    • the rturn of any stock can be separated into systematic (market) component and a residual.
    • CAPM assumes residual is zero.
    • there are other measures of expect returns.
      • Historical Average Returns
        • average return of stock over a period of time
        • this is a bad method
          • historical returns contain a large amount of sample error
          • the universe of stocks change over time
      • Abitrage Pricing Theory
        • this is useful but not considered the consensus expected return
    • the market portfolio is the consensus portfolio

      • Constructing CAPM
      • M = market portfolio
      • P = any portfolio
      • rp = portfolio P excess returns
      • rm = market portfolio M excess returns
      • βp = Cov(rp,rm)/Var(rm)
        • market excess return component
        • market portfolio has β = 1, risk-free assets have β = 0
      • αp =
      • εp =
      • θp = residual excess return component aka residual return
      • σ2p = β2pσ2M + ω2p
        • variance of portfolio P
      • ω2p = residual variance of portfolio P ie. variance of θ

        • CAPM formula

          rp = αp + βp*rm + εp
        • the α and β from the regression is the historical alpha and beta.
        • historical beta is a resonable forecast of beta that will be in the future.
        • We can now break excess return on P into a market component and residual component.
          • rp = βprm + θp
        • θp is the residual return and will be uncorrelated with market return
        • CAPM states that expected residual return is 0
        • this is due to assumption that all investors have same expectations and differ only on risk tolerance
      CAPM Discussion
      • CAPM says that some risks are nessesary and other risk are not.
      • market risk is nessesary, residual risk is self imposed

Risk
    Overview
    • risk is the standard deviation of return
    • the cost of risk is proportional to variance
    • risk models identify the important sources of risk and separate risk into components
    • need an operation and universal impersonal definition of risk
    • goal is to forecast risk
    Risk Measures
    • all definitions of risk arise fundamentally from the probability distribution of possible retunrs
    • goal is to understand the risk of the entire distribution by looking at 1 number
      • Standard Deviation
      • spread of the distribution around its mean
      • aka volitility
      • ** in normal distribution 2/3 of returns would lie in 1 standard devition of mean **
      • standard dev measures uncertaincy of return
      • you can forecast standard deviation
      • critism of stn dev
        • critics of stn dev say it measures returns above and below mean when really they only care about negative returns or small retunrs
        • alt risk measures have been developed
          • semivariance
            • like variance but only below the mean
            • if returns are semetric then semivariance is exactly one-half the variance
          • downside risk
            • square root of semivariance
          • target semivariance
            • semivariance around a defined target other than the mean
          • shortfall probability
            • probability that the return will lie below some target amount
            • example: probability of monthly loss in portfolio in book of 10% was 3.4%
            • hard to forecast
          • Value at Risk
            • example: worst 1% of all returns exceeded 20% loss
            • with this example, a $1000 investment in fund had VaR of $208
      • investment retunrs dont exactly follow normal distributions, the distributions are wider, ie probability of extreme events is higher

        • Calculating Standard Deveation of Portfolio
          σp = √((w1*σ1)2+ (w2*σ2)2 + 2 * (w1*σ1) * (w2*σ2) * ρ12)
        • Example: Portfolio with 3 stocks w/ equal weight
          • Asset Weight Expected Return
          1 50% 20%
          2 50% 30%
          corr .40

          σp = √((.5*.20)2 + (.5*.30)2 + 2 * (.5*.20) * (.50*.30) * .40)

          σp = 21.1%

        Annualizing Risk
          σannual = √12 * σmonthly

        Relative Risk
        • Active Return
        • portfolio returns rp are compared to the benchmark rB
        • this difference is called the Active Return
        • rPA = rp - rB

        • Active Risk
        • ψp = Std(rPA) = Std(rp - rB)
        • active risk is called tracking error
          • how well portfolio tracks the benchmark
        • **active risk can be proportional to the capitalization of the asset**
        • incorporate this into app
        • example: if IBM is 4% of benchmark, investor may set position limits of 2% on low side and 6% on high side
        • this limits exposure of IBM to +- 2% of benchmark
        • but active risk depends on active exposure and stock risk pg 50

        Residual Risk
        • risk of return orthogonal to the systematic return
        • example: residual risk of portfolio P relative to portfolio B (benchmark)
          • ωp = √(σ2p - β2p*σ2B)
        • βp = Cov(rp,rB)/Var(rB)

Risk Models
  • a portfolio of 2 stocks is easy to calculate but when you had 100s of assets its get more difficult

    • Covariance Matrix
    • summerizes all required estiamtes
  • the goal of a risk model is to accurately and efficently forecast the covariance matrix V

    • Simple Stock Risk Models

      Single factor diagonal model

      • each stock is assigned two components of risk
        • market risk
        • residual risk
      • returns are analyzed using:
          • rn = βn*rM + θn
        • βn = stock n beta
        • θn = stock n residual return
      • residual returns are correlated and must therefore for be negativly correlated (pg 53)
      • might expect positive correlation between stocks in same industry

      Risk Model 2

      • all pairs of stocks have the same correlation
      • requires an estimate of each stocks risk σ and correlation ρ between them
      • easy to compute and helpful for "quick and dirty applications"
      • ignores linkages between stocks in similar industries and firms w/ common atrributes

      Risk Model 3

      • full covariance model based on historical returns, variences, and covariances
      • historical approach requires T > N
      • historical data doesn't consider changing nature of companies, mergers, LBO, etc
    Structural Risk Models
    • multi - factor risk models is based on the notion that the return of a stock can be explained by
      • collection of common factors
      • indiosyncratic elements that pertains to that particular stock

        • ** by identifying important factors we can reduce the size of the problem

        instead of dealing with 6000 stocks and 18,003,000 independent variances, covariances, etc

        we deal with 68 factors

      • better to focus on a smaller number of factors and allow stocks to change their exposures to those factors
    • start model by analyzing returns according to simple linear structure (pg 55)
        • rn = ∑kXn,k * bk + un
      • rn = excess return on stock n
      • Xn,k = exposure of asset n to factor k. These are the X var inputs. standardize continus variables
      • bk = factor return to factor k
      • un = stock n specific return. return that cannot be explained by the factors. indiosyncratic return
      • **not a causal model**
      • returns should depict the change in value from t to t+1. one period change. could be monthly etc. then you just do the one period change back in history to get your estimation data
      • factors must reflect the begining of the period. ie. the first period in the change in value to get the return because you need the info a priori
      • Assume specific returns un are not correlated with factor returns bk
      • Assume specific returns un are not correlated with each other
    • Once you have linear models for all assets decribed above you need to calulate the covariance matrix V of asset n and asset m
        • V n,m = ∑k Xn,k1 * F * X m,k2 + Δn,m

      • Active Variance (fotnote pg 116)
          • ψ2P = hTPA * V * hPA
        • ψ2 = active variance
        • hTPA
        • hPA = hP - hB
          • hP and hB are the holding in the mangaged and benchmark portfolios
      • The Risk models analyzes current portfolio risk
      • python/pseudo code to get to this point
        • get excess returns from 2 assets (t - t-1) and the factors for the begining of the period.

Exceptional Returns, Benchmarks, & Added Value

Overview
  • CAPM provides consensus expected returns
  • multi-factor model can help control risk
  • now we need accurate forecasts of expected retunrs

Benchmarks
  • active managers can be asked to outperform a benchmark that is not "the market"
  • Active Return si the difference between return on the manger portfolio and return on the benchmark
  • We can still use beta from CAPM
  • β = Cov(rnrb)/Var(rb)
  • Active Variance

Expected Return
  • if = time premium → risk free component
    • compensation for time. we know return on risk free aset so we can assign premium in advance
  • βn * μB = risk premium → benchmark component
    • μB = consensus expected excess return on benchmark
    • forecasted at a very long time
  • βn * ΔfB = exceptional benchmark return → benchmark timing component
    • μB is forecasted at a very long time, if you think a short time is different that look at ΔfB
    • ΔfB = differnece between expected excess return on the benchmark in near future and the long run
  • αn = expected residual return → alpha
    • αn = E{θn}
  • Rn = total return on asset n
  • fn = βn * μB + βn * ΔfB + αn
    • Expected excess return.
    • fB = ∑n hB(n) * E{Rn - (1 + i F)}
    • hB(n) = weight of each asset in the benchmark
    • ΔfB = fB - μB

    E{Rn} = 1 + if + βn * μB + βn * ΔfB + αn

  • Exceptional expected return
    • βn * ΔfB + αn
    • ** THis is the key to active managment
    • βn * ΔfB measures benchmark timing
      • this generates a single bet for or against the benchmark
    • αn measures stock selection
  • when modeling historical alphas and betas can be used to forecat expected return given the other paramenters

Total Risk vs Total Return
  • Efficient frontier
    • Y axis = Expected Excess Return
    • X Axis = Risk
    Forecast of expected excess returns
    • **Active management starts when the managers forecast differs from the consensus**
      • fn = βn * fB + αn
      • fn = forecast of expected excess return for stock n
      • fB = forecast of expected excess return for the benchmark. fB differs from μB → the consensus estimate
      • βn = stock n beta
      • αn = stock n forecast alpha. alpha is zero in consensus model
    Expected Utility
    • this is the bases for choosing a portfolio since it is dependednt on risk rward trade off that is unquie to everyone
    • Expected Utility function
        • U[P] = fp - λT* σ2P
      • fp = expected excess return
      • λT = μB / 2 * σ2B
        • if f = μ (forecast return = consensus return), ie. no new information relative to bbenchmark then λT = μB / 2 * σ2B
        • aversion to total risk. both systematic (benchmark) and residual (asset selection) risk
        • other values for risk aversion include 1/λ and λ/2
        • Goal is to find eligible portfolio with highest possible expected utility
      • σ2P = β2P * σ2B
        • σ2B = variance of benchmark
        • I think this function is correct not 100% positive
    • Optimal Portfolio
      • the optimal portfolio is the portfolio on the frontier that has highest risk adjusted returns
        • Holdings (weights) of optimal portfolio (pg 98)
        • hp = (fQ/2 * λT*σ2Q) * hQ = βP * hB + (IR/2 * λT) * hA
        • IR = information ratio → measures expected residual returns to residual risk
    • expected utility criteria will lead to portfolios that are too aggressive.
    • typical level of risk aversion = 0.0075
    • example return data:
      • fp = 27.29%
      • σP = 25.20%, total risk
      • βP = 1.05
      • αP = 20.99%
      • ωP = 13.78%, this is the residal risk
      • **This is an agressive portfolio, need to get results more inline with institutional practice → need to focus on active component of return and look at active risk reward

    Value Added
      Risk of Active Manager
    • where as above are the components of the returns these are the correponding components of the risk. They have similar features.
      • Intrinsic = fb - λT*σ2B
        • risk / return of the benchmark. not under managers control
      • Timing = βPA * ΔfB - λBT*β2PA*σ2B
        • risk of timing the benchmark.
        • different risk aversion component
      • Residual = αP - λR*ω2P
        • risk to managers residual position
      • VA = Timing + Residual
        • Value Added
            • VA = {βPA * ΔfB - λBT*β2PA*σ2B} + {αP - λR*ω2P}
      • The framework is going to mostly be ignoreing market timing and therefore asuming the risk that goes with it. Authors give reason why

      The Objective of Value Added Management
      • Start with three items
        • forecast of returns f
        • portfolio holding/weights hp
        • portfolio variance σ2p

The Information Ratio
    Overview
    • the IR is the notion of the active manger success
    • Ex Ante vs Ex Post IR
      • the Ex Ante IR is connotes oppurtunity
      • the Ex Post IR measures achievment

    Defining Alpha
    • Ex post, alpha is the average of the realized residual return
      • rP = αP + βP rB + εP
      • θP = αP +εP
      • in ex post framework alpha and beta are realized/historical alpha beta
      • realized alphas are for keeping score
    • Ex ante, alpha is a forecast of residual retunrs

      • Alpha of portfolio
      • assume two stocks with α1 and α2
      • if we have two stock portfolio w/ holding hP1 and hP2 then alpha of portfolio is
        • αP = hP1 * α1 + hP2 * α2
    • alpha of benchmark αB = 0
    • alpha of risk free αF = 0
    The Information Ratio
      IR = αP/ ωP
    • ratio of annualized residual return to annualized residual risk
    • at top quality IR is 0.5 or above
  • Active Holding = portfolio holding - benckmark holdings
  • Active Variance (fotnote pg 116)
      • ψ2P = hTPA * V * hPA
    • ψ2 = active variance
    • hTPA
    • hPA = hP - hB
      • hP and hB are the holding in the mangaged and benchmark portfolios
  • IR depends on time horizon
    • return grows with time
    • risk (standard deviation) will grow as the sqrt of horizon
    • quarterly IR is half the annual IR
    • monthly IR is 1/&sqrt;12 the size of annual IR

    Aggresivness vs Oppurtunity and optimal risk
    • graph resudual frontier pg 117
    • value added cureve supper imposed on the residual frontier (pg 120)
    • **formal problem is to max α - λ * ω2 subject to the constraint α/ω ≤ IR
      • ω* = IR/(2*λR)
      • optimal level of risk

    Optimal Portfolio
    • VA* = (ω* * IR)/2
Fundemental law of Active Management
  • this law give a very good approximation to the information ratio
  • BR = breadth. number of independent forecasts of exceptional returns made each year
  • IC = information coefficient, skill/measures the quality of investment decisions. the correlation of each forecast w/ actual outcomes
  • IR = IC * √BR
  • new optimal level of risk
      • ω* = IC * √BR/(2*λR)
  • new value added
      • VA* = (IC2 * BR)/4λR
  • law is designed to give insight into active management → not operational tool
