Supply
  • estimating the country's potential output
    • good estimates of potential output are used to calculate the output gap and projecting GDP 3-5 years out.
    Potential Output
    • Theoretical Form
      • Y = Af(K,L)
        • Y = aggregate real output
        • K = index of capital input per year
        • L = hours worked per year
        • A = total factor productivity (TFP). Often calucalated as residual
    • Functional Form
      • Y = A*Kα*L1-α
        • Y = aggregate real output
        • K = index of capital input per year
        • L = hours worked per year
        • A = total factor productivity (TFP). Often calucalated as residual
        • α = proportionate contribution of K to total input, usually assumed to be 0.3
    • Assumptions
      • Constant Returns to scale = this is important because it means firms of any size can compete.
      • MPK and MPL are positive
      • second derivative is negative
    • Estimating Potential Output
      • Advanced Countries
        • advanced countries have more capital per worker, higher K/L and better technology
        • A = Y/Kα*L1-α
        • A must be calcuated by residual
        • Data for L is from hours of working aged population that would be worked if a country werre able to employ its working-age population
        • Data for K reflects the capital services used during the period but are proxied by the actual nonresidential capital stock.
        • the future path of potential output per worker depends on higher K/L (capital deepening)
        • Capitak Stock can be found where ?????
        • the time series need to be cyclically adjusted → CBO used unemplyment rate to adjust.
        • using a trend line on real GDP times series is a good approximation of potential output.
        • sudden rise in A could be a bubble instead of true growth in productivity. Cite GFC in Britan as example
      • Developing Countries

        • Countries closed to foriegn capital

    Real GDP
    • Income-Production Approach
    • Aggregate Expenditure Approach
      • everything produced leads to expenditure
        • GDP = C + IF + IN + X - M
      • C = consumption by private sector and government
      • IF = fixed investment by private sector and government
      • IN = inventory investment
      • X = exports
      • M = imports
    • analysts are interested in strength of domestic demand, because domestic countercyclical measures have some degree of influence
    • therfore GDP can be re-written as
        • GDP = TDD + FB
      • TDD = total domestic demand (C+IF+IN)
      • FB = foreign balance (X-M)
    • total domestica demand breaks down into final domestic demand and inventory accumulation
    • inventory is normal low and can go negative
    • significant inventory accumulation (decumulation) signals a downturn (upturn) in GDP
        • TDD = FDD + IN
      • FDD = Final Domenstic Demand (C + IF)
      • IN = inventory
    • FDD can be broken down into private sector and government
        • FDD = Cp + IFp + Cg + IFg
      • Cp = private consumption
      • IFp = private fixed investment
      • Cg = government consumption
      • IFg = government fixed investment
    • if Cp and IFp are weak, Cg and IFg may need to be raised
      • Consumption
      • nondurable consumption
      • durable consumption
      Investment
        Fixed Investment
        • spending on machinery & euipment
        • spending on buildings and infrastructure
        Inventory Investment
        • spending on inventories
    GNI/GNP
    • GDP covers trade in goods and services. it does not cover other sources of income from abroad or income paid to non residents
      • GNI = GDP + NFI
    • NFI = net factor income form abroad
      • net income from labor services
        • earnings from domestic residents working abroad - payments to foreign residents working domestically
      • net income from capital services
        • interest on loans made to foriengers - interest paid on foreign borrowing
    • for countries with a lot of remittances by residents working abroad the difference between GDP and GNI can be large
      • Examples:
        • Ireland has GDP > GNI because it send much income/profits abroad
        • Kuwait, oil exporter, has GNI > GDP because it has a lot of investments abroad and thus large interest earnings from foriegn investment
    • Gross National Disposable Income is GNI augmented by net transfers from abroad
      • GNDI = GNI + TR
    • Gross National Disposibale Inceom
    • TR = net transfers from abroad unrealted to factor earnings
      • public foreign aid
      • private philanthropy
    • this relates to the external accounts through the Current Account Balances and the BOP
      • CAB = X - M + NFI + TR
Real Resource Constraint (RRC)
  • a rearranged version of GNDI
  • domestic investment, which builds the capital stock for future production is constrained by resources available from domestic savings and foreign saving channled to domestic economy
  • RRC is the summary expression for the total amount of resources available for domestic investment
    • GNDI = C + I + CAB
    • I = IF + IN
  • Gross National Savings is GNDI - C
    • S = GNDI - C = I + CAB
    • S = Gross National Savings
  • Therefore the RRC,total amount of resources available for domestic investment, is I
    • I = S - CAB
    • expressing CAB as surplus
    I = S + CAB
    • expressing CAB as deficit
    • CAD = -CAB = current account deficit
    Tools for analysis
  • Fiscal
  • For fiscal policy analysis, you can disaggregate savings
    • I = Sp + Sg + CAD
    • Sp = private savings
    • Sg = government savings
  • you can focus on Ip by removing Ig from both sides of equation
    • this says that a drop in government surplus (rise in gov defcit) must be matched by either a reduction in private investment or an increase in external current account, or higher private savings
    Ip = Sp + GS + CAD
    • Ip = total private investment (fixed + inventory)
    • GS = government surplus = Sg - Ig
  • Demand
  • FOr total domenstic demand analysis, TDD = C + IF + IN
    • TDD is refered to as absorption in context of RRC
    • total consumption + investment
      • CAD = CDA - CNDI
      • GDA = TDD = domestic absorption
      • the CAD is the difference between expenditure (absorption) and income

Examples
  • Foreign Inflow Shock
    • **** official aid to low income countries are reflected in the form of current transfers
    • goal of transfer is to raise per capita GDP
    • Once transfer to the foreign government is complete
      • Ip = Sp + GS↑ + CAD↓
      • if money is spent on imports (medicine/tanks)
        • GS and CAD go back to orignal level
      • if money is spent on domestic non-traded goods that can't be imported (ie road building)
        • GS rises with inflow but goes back to normal level
        • CAD goes down with inflow and stays down. no off set
        • with output gap remaing balance is taken up by higher savings, more private investment, and imports from workers on the project
        • with no output gap higher spending creates hire wages and prices
  • Current Account Shock
    • large increse in current account deficit
    • if country is able to borrow on international financial markets to finance a larger deficit may not be an issue (case for USA)
    • this senario assumes that debt is already high and exchange rates are not allowed to respond fully to market forces
    • CAD using BOP approach: focused on trade and income flows
      • CAD = M - X - NFI - TR
      • identify which component of the deficit had moved sharply
    • CAD using absoption approach
      • CAD = ABS - GNDI
    • CAD using absoption approach: output and absorption
      • CAD = (Ip - Sp) + (Ig - Sg) = (Ip - Sp) - GS
      • this happens when CAD has clear counterpart in rising absoportion
      • determine if this was due to changes in government policy or cyclical conditions in private sector
    • CAD using resource constrain approach approach: private and government I-S balances
      • CAD = (Ip - Sp) + (Ig - Sg) = (Ip - Sp) - GS
